Security & compliance

Cortex processes protected health information (PHI) under a signed Business Associate Agreement. Every claim on this page maps to a control you can exercise in the live product — the permission matrix, the redaction engine, and the tamper-evident audit trail are all demonstrable in the healthcare demo.

Last updated 21 July 2026 · Cortex is a demonstration product built by team Cloud9.

✔ Encrypted in transit

TLS 1.3 everywhere, mTLS on clinical system connectors. No plaintext PHI crosses a network boundary.

✔ Encrypted at rest

AES-256-GCM with per-tenant keys, 90-day rotation, HSM-backed root, optional BYOK.

✔ HIPAA & BAA

BAA executed before any PHI is transmitted. HIPAA Security Rule safeguards implemented and evidenced.

✔ Never trained on your data

Customer PHI is never used to train, fine-tune, or evaluate models. Contractual and technical.

Encryption

PHI is encrypted in transit and at rest at all times. Encryption is not configurable per tenant — it cannot be turned down or opted out of.

In transit — external TLS 1.3 (TLS 1.2 permitted only for legacy clinical interfaces on request). Cipher suites limited to TLS_AES_256_GCM_SHA384 and TLS_CHACHA20_POLY1305_SHA256. HSTS enforced with a two-year max-age and preload.
In transit — internal Mutual TLS between all services. Connectors to EHR, LIS, and scheduling systems require client-certificate authentication; certificates rotate every 90 days.
At rest AES-256-GCM for all PHI in databases, object storage, and backups. Disk-level encryption in addition to application-level field encryption for direct identifiers.
Key management Per-tenant data encryption key (DEK) wrapped by a per-tenant key encryption key (KEK), rooted in an HSM. No cross-tenant key grants exist. Automatic rotation every 90 days. Customer-managed keys (BYOK) available via KMS grant, including the ability to revoke access unilaterally.
Backups Encrypted with the same per-tenant key hierarchy. 35-day retention. Restores tested quarterly.
Key access No Cortex employee can export a tenant key. Break-glass key access requires two-person authorization and writes an immutable audit record visible to the customer.

HIPAA and the Business Associate Agreement

Cortex acts as a Business Associate under HIPAA. A BAA is executed before any PHI is transmitted; sandbox tenants carry a technical no-PHI restriction until it is countersigned.

RoleBusiness Associate (45 CFR Parts 160 and 164). Covered Entity retains ownership of all PHI at all times.
BAA statusRequired and executed before PHI transmission. Counter-signature gates the production tenant; it is enforced in provisioning, not by policy alone.
Security Rule safeguards Administrative, physical, and technical safeguards per §164.308, §164.310, and §164.312 — including access control (§164.312(a)), audit controls (§164.312(b)), integrity controls (§164.312(c)), and transmission security (§164.312(e)).
Minimum necessary Enforced in the interface: PHI is redacted by default and revealed per-field against a recorded permitted purpose. See PHI handling.
Breach notification Notification to the Covered Entity without unreasonable delay and no later than 15 calendar days after discovery (BAA §6.3); 24-hour preliminary notice for confirmed PHI exposure. See incident response.
Return or destruction On termination, all PHI returned or destroyed within 30 days, with a certificate of destruction on request (BAA §9.1).
Subcontractors Every subcontractor with potential PHI access is bound by a written agreement no less restrictive than this BAA. See subprocessors.
Key BAA clauses. §3.2 Permitted Uses — Business Associate shall not use or disclose PHI other than as permitted by the Agreement or as Required by Law. §4.1 Safeguards — implement safeguards that reasonably and appropriately protect the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of electronic PHI. §7.4 No Secondary Use — PHI shall not be used to train, fine-tune, or evaluate machine learning models. Full executed copy available under NDA — see contact.

PHI handling in the interface

Minimum necessary is enforced by the UI, not left to user discipline. This is directly exercisable in the demo.

Default stateRedacted. Direct identifiers are masked in every view before render.
DetectorsName, MRN, date of birth, SSN, phone, email, address, insurance ID.
Context preservation Only the identifier itself is replaced; surrounding clinical and operational text is passed through unchanged, so records remain readable. Patient Smith SSN 123-45-6789 redacts to Patient [NAME REDACTED] SSN [SSN REDACTED] — the word "Patient" and the label "SSN" are preserved as non-sensitive context.
Controlled reveal Per-field, and requires: the reveal_phi capability, a HIPAA permitted-purpose reason code, a written justification of at least 20 characters, and step-up authentication. Reveals are time-boxed to 60 seconds and re-redact automatically.
Step-up authentication Enforced by your identity provider (SAML/OIDC); Cortex verifies the AMR/ACR claim and records it. Cortex stores no passwords and issues no MFA factors of its own.
Logging Every reveal, expiry, and denied attempt is written to the audit trail with the actor, field, reason code, and justification verbatim. Raw identifier values are never logged.
ExportsRedacted unless an explicit unmasked-export approval is on file.

Access control

Least privilege by default. Capabilities are granted per role and denied unless explicitly held; denied attempts are themselves audited.

ModelRole-based access control with capability-level granularity. No role holds a wildcard permission.
AuthenticationFederated via SAML 2.0 or OIDC to your IdP. No local passwords. SCIM provisioning supported; deprovisioning propagates in under five minutes.
Automation ceiling Automation cannot outrun oversight. Low-risk reversible actions may auto-execute above 0.90 confidence; medium risk requires a human approval; high risk requires two distinct approvers with no self-countersignature; anything flagged patient-safety is never auto-executed regardless of confidence.
ReversibilityEvery executed action carries one-click rollback, which writes a paired audit entry. A tenant administrator can suspend all automation with a single control.
Vendor accessNo standing Cortex employee access to tenant PHI. Break-glass support access requires customer approval, is time-boxed, and is logged to the customer's own audit trail.

Tenant data segregation

Separation is structural, not a row filter.

StorageDedicated database schema per tenant. Not shared tables with row-level filtering.
Object storagePer-tenant prefix with per-tenant encryption key. No cross-tenant key grant exists.
Query enforcementTenant identity is asserted from the signed session and enforced at the data-access layer. Statements without a tenant predicate are rejected at the driver.
Continuous testingA nightly job attempts a cross-tenant read and expects denial. Any success pages the on-call security engineer.
EgressPer-tenant allowlist. PHI leaving an approved boundary is quarantined and escalated rather than transmitted.

Model and data use

Your data is used to serve you, and for nothing else.

Training on customer dataNever. Prohibited by contract (§7.4) and enforced technically — inference is stateless with no gradient updates.
Fine-tuning or evaluationNever, including for aggregate or de-identified model improvement.
Third-party model APIsNo PHI is sent to any third-party model provider. Inference runs inside the tenant boundary.
Human reviewNo vendor employee reviews tenant data without an approved, logged break-glass grant.
Prompt / response retentionNot retained. Audit metadata is retained; raw identifier values are not.
Retention & deletionAudit records retained per tenant policy, default seven years. Deletion requests honored within 30 days with a certificate of destruction.
ExplainabilityEvery model decision exposes its confidence, weighted contributing signals, the policy that fired, counter-evidence, and full lineage from source system to proposed action.

Audit trail

Compliance-grade and tamper-evident. Verification is a feature of the product, not an assurance in a document.

CoverageEvery human action, model decision, PHI reveal, denied access attempt, export, and rollback.
Record contentsSequence, UTC timestamp, actor, role, action, object, risk tier, inputs, outputs, justification, lineage, previous hash, and hash.
IntegrityHash-chained: each entry commits to its predecessor. Any edit breaks verification at that row. Production additionally writes to WORM storage with a daily anchored digest.
Customer verificationChain verification is exposed in the product. The demo includes a deliberate tampering simulation so you can watch detection occur.
ExportCSV and JSON, scoped to the filtered view, with the verification result embedded in the JSON payload.

Incident response & breach notification

Severity 1 — confirmed PHI exposure Acknowledged within 30 minutes. Preliminary customer notification within 24 hours; formal notification within 15 days per BAA §6.3. Forensics package within 10 business days.
Severity 2 — suspected exposure Acknowledged within 2 hours, status updates every 12 hours until closed.
On-call24/7 security on-call rotation.
ExercisesSemi-annual tabletop exercises, customer-observable on request.
PostmortemsBlameless, and shared with affected customers.
